teaching philosophy

What you're actually teaching

The real goal of this module

You are not teaching coding in this module. You are teaching a way of seeing problems. The goal is that by Lesson 6, a student can look at any challenge — technical or not — and approach it with structured curiosity rather than panic or avoidance.

This is harder to measure than "can they write a for loop" but it's far more valuable. The students who develop this mindset will outperform those who just learn syntax, every single time.

TEACHING TIP

You don't need to know everything. In fact, modelling "I don't know — let's find out" is one of the most powerful things you can do. Students learn that not knowing is the starting point, not a failure.

The design process as your anchor

Every lesson in this module can be tied back to the design process: research → define → ideate → prototype → test. When students feel lost, bring them back to "which step are we on?" This gives them a map, not just a destination.

The connection to art and design is intentional and real — not a metaphor. Students with design backgrounds have an enormous advantage in software because they already think in systems and feedback loops. Honour that.

Welcome & How This Works

Orientation, expectations, and the learning contract

15 min orientation

Purpose of this session

This is not a content session — it's a relationship session. Your goal is for every student to feel like this is a place where it's safe to not know things. Set the tone for the entire module here.

Suggested script

// OPENING — SAY SOMETHING LIKE THIS

"Before we learn anything, I want to be upfront with you. I'm not going to pretend I know everything about AI and coding — nobody does right now, including people who've been doing it for 20 years. What I can do is show you how to think about problems and how to use the tools that exist. The rest we figure out together."

Logistics to cover

  • How the portal works (show them the sidebar navigation)
  • How to access the Student Workbook PDF
  • What "marking complete" does (tracks their own progress)
  • That the access code is theirs alone — not for sharing
  • When sessions are, how to reach you between sessions
GROUP SETTING TIP

If teaching a group, start with a quick round: each student says their name + one thing they've built or created (anything — a cake, a playlist, a sketch). This frames everyone as already being a creator.

What is a Builder's Mindset?

The three questions, the design process connection, and the first activity

40 min mindset
+

Session timing

Intro (5 min)
Core concept (10 min)
Activity (20 min)
Debrief (5 min)

What to emphasise

  • The mindset is a skill, not a personality trait — learnable by everyone
  • The design process connection is central, not supplementary
  • "I don't know yet" is the correct starting position for every problem
  • Freelancers get paid for their thinking as much as their building

Discussion questions (choose 1–2)

Can you think of a time a problem seemed impossible until someone broke it into smaller parts?

What's the difference between a person who gives up on a bug and one who keeps going?

If a client told you "build me something cool" — what would you do first?

THE THREE QUESTIONS IN PRACTICE

During the activity debrief, pick one student's "frustration" and walk through the three questions out loud with the group. Model the thinking process in real time. This is more valuable than any explanation.

COMMON STUDENT MISTAKE

Students pick a "frustration" that's too abstract (e.g. "the world is unfair"). Redirect them: "What's something that took you longer than it should have this week?" Make it concrete and personal.

Activity debrief questions

What was the "smallest version" you came up with? Is it actually buildable?

How did it feel to work backwards from the solution?

02

Patterns Everywhere

Maths as pattern recognition, the art connection, and the Fibonacci activity

45 min patterns · maths
+

Session timing

Intro (5 min)
Core concept (10 min)
Activity (25 min)
Debrief (5 min)

Why this lesson matters

This lesson does two things. First, it reframes maths for students who think they "can't do maths" — because pattern recognition is something everyone does naturally. Second, it introduces the concept that AI models work by detecting patterns, which makes the student a much better AI user.

The Fibonacci connection (handle carefully)

The Fibonacci sequence (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8...) is a good entry point because students often already know it from biology or art. The rule is: each number = the sum of the two before it. The point isn't the maths — it's that once you see the rule, you can predict anything in the sequence, and write code to generate it.

FOR STUDENTS WHO THINK THEY'RE BAD AT MATHS

Ask them: "Can you recognise when a song has a chorus? When a movie follows a formula?" That's pattern recognition. It's the same thing. Maths anxiety comes from calculation, not from pattern-spotting. Keep the focus on seeing, not solving.

The art + design connection to emphasise

  • Grid systems in graphic design are mathematical structures
  • Colour theory (complementary colours, triads) uses proportional relationships
  • Good typography uses ratio and repetition — mathematical principles
  • Any student who did visual design has already been doing this
COMMON STUDENT MISTAKE

Students find patterns on a website (e.g. "the nav has links") but don't go deep enough. Push them: "Why are there exactly that many columns? What stays constant as you scroll? What rule makes the spacing feel right?" Precision in observation transfers directly to precision in code.

03

Design Thinking Meets Code

The design process as a freelance framework, the bakery brief activity

45 min design · freelance
+

Session timing

Intro (5 min)
Core concept (10 min)
Activity (25 min)
Debrief (5 min)

The key insight to land

Students who've done design already have a professional edge — they just haven't connected it to software yet. Your job is to make that connection explicit and show them it's not incidental, it's central. The best developers are design thinkers.

The bakery scenario — how to run it

  • Read the bakery scenario aloud as a group first
  • Ask students: "What's missing from this brief?" — let them surface the gaps
  • Then have them write their 5 questions independently
  • Compare questions in the debrief — interesting how different they are
FOR GROUPS — ROLE PLAY IT

Have one student play the bakery owner and another play the developer. Run a 5-minute "discovery call." The bakery owner should be vague and unsure — that's realistic. The developer asks questions. This usually generates great discussion about what clients actually need vs. what they say they want.

Australian context note

The bakery is in Brisbane deliberately. Encourage students to think about real small businesses near them — a local café, a surf school, a tutoring centre. Freelance clients often come from your immediate network first. Make it feel real and local.

COMMON STUDENT MISTAKE

Students jump to "I'd use WordPress" or "I'd build a React app" before they've understood the problem. Gently redirect: "Before we choose tools, do we know enough about what they actually need?" This is the most important professional habit to build early.

04

Breaking Problems Into Pieces

Decomposition, AI prompting precision, the tree diagram activity

45 min decomposition · ai
+

Session timing

Intro (5 min)
Core concept (10 min)
Activity (25 min)
Debrief (5 min)

Core concept: decomposition

Decomposition is not a coding skill — it's a thinking skill that happens before any code is written. Students who can decompose well will debug faster, prompt better, and deliver cleaner work to clients. This lesson is arguably the most practically important in the module.

The AI connection is critical

Spend time here. The reason vague prompts produce bad output is that the AI can't decompose the problem for you without context. When you decompose first, your prompt becomes specific — and specific prompts produce dramatically better results. This is a skill most adult developers haven't learnt. Your students will be ahead of them.

LIVE DEMO — DO THIS IN SESSION

Open Claude in front of the class. First, enter: "Build me an app." Show the output. Then enter a properly decomposed, specific prompt for the same idea. Show the difference. This is the most convincing demonstration possible — seeing is believing.

Tree diagram — what to look for

  • Students should go at least 3 levels deep (app → feature → component)
  • Leaves of the tree should be small enough to describe in one sentence
  • Good sign: the micro-prompt they write sounds like something a developer would actually say
  • Concern: if their smallest pieces are still "make the whole login system" — go deeper
05

Your First AI Conversation

Prompt formula, hands-on Claude session, the bakery prompt activity

45 min practical · ai
+

Session timing

Intro (5 min)
Core concept (10 min)
Activity (25 min)
Debrief (5 min)

Before this session

  • Check every student has a free Claude account (claude.ai)
  • Confirm internet is stable — this lesson requires live AI access
  • Have a backup activity ready (write prompts on paper, test later) if connectivity fails

The prompt formula — keep it simple

Context → Task → Constraints → Format. That's it. Don't overcomplicate. Students will develop intuition for when to use each element over time. Right now, the habit of adding context before asking is the most important shift.

WHEN AI GIVES A WRONG OR WEIRD ANSWER

This will happen. Treat it as a teaching moment, not a failure: "Interesting — why do you think it said that? What context did we not give it?" This builds critical AI literacy, which is exactly what makes a great developer: someone who uses AI well and knows when it's wrong.

The multi-turn conversation is the point

Most students will type one message and read the response passively. Push them to reply: "Too long — make it simpler." "What would this cost?" "Can you show me a different approach?" The back-and-forth is where the real learning happens. AI as a conversation, not a search engine.

COMMON STUDENT MISTAKE

Students copy the AI's output without reading it. Ask them: "Does this actually solve the bakery problem? Read it back to me in your own words." If they can't, they haven't engaged with the output critically. This is the most important meta-skill of the AI era.

The Freelance Mindset

What freelancing is, the freelancer path, final module activity

45 min freelance · careers
+

Session timing

Intro (5 min)
Core concept (10 min)
Activity (25 min)
Debrief (5 min)

Set the tone honestly

Freelancing is not passive income — it's real work that requires real skills. But it's also completely accessible to a motivated high school student. The path from "learning to code" to "first paid client" can be as short as six months with the right approach. Don't oversell, don't undersell. Be real.

The freelancer shadowing opportunity

Mention this explicitly. High-performing students who complete Phase 1 and 2 will get connected to working freelancers — not big companies, but real independent developers. This is something most students will never get access to through traditional school. Frame it as a reward for staying committed.

AUSTRALIAN CONTEXT — USE THIS

Australian small businesses are a massive market. There are over 2 million small businesses in Australia, most of which have weak or no digital presence. The demand for affordable freelance web/app work from local developers is real and growing. Your students are building toward a real opportunity, not a fantasy.

Final activity — what to look for in their pitches

  • Is the pitch specific about who they help? (Not "anyone" — that's too vague)
  • Is the potential client they named real and reachable?
  • Is their price range realistic? (First clients: $100–$500 for a simple site is normal)
  • Does the workbook reflection show genuine shift in thinking?

Module close — what to say

"You've just done something most adults haven't done — you've started thinking about how to build things with AI deliberately, not accidentally. Phase 1 is where the actual building starts. You'll write real code, ship a real project, and put it on a real URL. Everything you practised thinking about in this module, you're about to start making real. I'll see you there."

general teaching notes

Running the sessions

Group sessions vs. 1-on-1

Groups (4–6 students): Run the discussion questions as a full group debrief. Pair students for activities where possible — two people decomposing a problem together is closer to real work than solo. Use the bakery roleplay in Lesson 3.

1-on-1: Go deeper on whatever interests the student most. If they're drawn to the design connection, spend more time there. If they're excited about AI, start the Claude session in Lesson 4 instead of waiting for Lesson 5. Follow their energy.

Managing different skill levels

  • Total beginners: Keep the Fibonacci activity gentle — the rule discovery is enough. Don't push into code notation.
  • Students with some coding experience: Challenge them to connect each lesson to a project they've tried before. Where did they skip the design thinking? Where did decomposition break down?
  • Overconfident students: Gently introduce ambiguity. "Okay, now explain why that specific solution — not just what it does." Confidence without understanding is the biggest risk.
